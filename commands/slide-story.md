## Instructions

Build a presentation outline that tells a story instead of presenting a list of facts. Use the SCR (Situation, Complication, Resolution) narrative framework to structure the entire talk.

This command is for presentations where the goal is to change how the audience thinks or feels about a topic, not just inform them. Use it for proposals, case studies, lessons learned, and persuasion talks.

### Narrative Structure

#### Act 1: Situation (15-20% of total time)
Set the scene. Describe the world as the audience knows it. Establish common ground.
- What is the current state?
- What does the audience already believe or experience?
- Use concrete details: names, dates, metrics, places. Specifics build credibility.

#### Act 2: Complication (30-40% of total time)
Introduce the problem, tension, or change that disrupts the status quo. This is the core of the story.
- What went wrong, changed, or was discovered?
- Why do existing approaches fall short?
- Build tension. The audience should feel the weight of the problem before you offer the solution.
- Use real examples, failed attempts, or "we tried X and here is what happened" moments.

#### Act 3: Resolution (30-40% of total time)
Present the solution, outcome, or new understanding. Pay off the tension from Act 2.
- What did you build, change, or learn?
- Show the results with data.
- Be honest about tradeoffs and what is still not solved.

#### Closing: The Takeaway (5-10% of total time)
Land the plane with a single, memorable message.
- One sentence the audience will repeat to their team tomorrow.
- A clear call to action.

### Slide Design Within the Story

Follow these rules for every slide:

1. **No more than 4 bullet points per slide.** If you need more, split into two slides.
2. **Each bullet point should be under 12 words.**
3. **One idea per slide.**
4. **Story slides should be mostly visual.** A photo, a diagram, or a single powerful stat displayed large.
5. **Use "chapter title" slides** to signal transitions between Act 1, Act 2, and Act 3. These can be a single sentence on a dark background.

### Speaker Notes for Story Presentations

Speaker notes for narrative presentations are different from standard talks:
- Write them as if the presenter is telling the story to a friend. Conversational, not formal.
- Include the emotional beat for each slide: "This is where the audience should feel the frustration of the old system."
- Include specific transition phrases that maintain narrative flow: "But then something changed..." or "And that is when we realized..."
- Note where to slow down for impact and where to speed up through setup details.

## Output Format

### Title Slide
- Title (should hint at the story, not just state the topic)
- Subtitle with speaker name placeholder and event placeholder
- Suggested timing: 30 seconds

### Act 1: Situation
- 2-4 slides setting the scene
- Each slide with title, 2-4 bullet points, speaker notes, and visual suggestion

### Act 2: Complication
- 4-8 slides building the tension
- Each slide with title, 2-4 bullet points, speaker notes, and visual suggestion

### Act 3: Resolution
- 4-8 slides delivering the payoff
- Each slide with title, 2-4 bullet points, speaker notes, and visual suggestion

### Closing
- Key takeaway slide
- Call to action slide

### Q&A Preparation
List 5-7 anticipated questions with answers. For story-driven talks, expect questions like "What would you do differently?" and "How does this apply to our situation?"

### Pacing Summary
A table showing slide number, title, act, and time allocation. Verify the times add up to the total time requested. Flag any slide over 3 minutes.
